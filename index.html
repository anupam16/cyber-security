Introduction to Cyber Security
Simple Analogy: Think of your house. You lock the doors (physical security). Cyber security is the same — but for your computer, data, and networks. It's about keeping the "bad guys" out of your digital house.

What is Cyber Security?

Cyber Security is the practice of protecting computers, servers, mobile devices, networks, and data from digital attacks, damage, or unauthorized access.

It covers everything from protecting your personal laptop to securing a country's power grid.

Why is Cyber Security Important?

  • We store everything digitally — bank details, medical records, personal photos.
  • Cyber attacks cost the world trillions of dollars every year.
  • A single breach can destroy a company's reputation overnight.
  • Critical systems (hospitals, power grids, banks) depend on secure networks.

Information Security Principles (CIA Triad)
Simple Analogy: CIA Triad is the "3 golden rules" of security. Imagine a bank locker:
— Only you can open it (Confidentiality)
— The money inside must not be tampered with (Integrity)
— The locker must be accessible whenever you need it (Availability)

What is the CIA Triad?

The CIA Triad is the foundation of information security. Every security decision is based on these three principles.

1. Confidentiality — "Only the right people can see it"

What it is: Ensuring that information is accessible only to those authorized to see it. Unauthorized people must be kept out.

How it works: Through encryption, passwords, access controls, and authentication.

MethodHow it ensures Confidentiality
EncryptionScrambles data so only someone with the key can read it (like a secret code)
Access ControlOnly gives access to people who should have it (e.g., HR can see salaries, others can't)
Passwords / Multi-Factor AuthVerifies identity before allowing access
Data ClassificationLabels data as Public / Internal / Confidential / Secret
Confidentiality Measure = Authorized Access / Total Access Attempts
• Authorized Access = number of times legitimate users successfully accessed data • Total Access Attempts = all access attempts (including unauthorized ones) • Result closer to 1.0 = better confidentiality

Threats to Confidentiality: Eavesdropping, data breaches, insider threats, shoulder surfing, phishing.

2. Integrity — "The data must not be tampered with"

What it is: Ensuring that data is accurate, complete, and has not been modified without authorization.

How it works: Hashing, digital signatures, checksums, version control.

Hash Function: H(M) = Fixed-length output (digest)
• H = Hash function (e.g., SHA-256, MD5) • M = Message or file (the original data) • Fixed-length output (digest) = a unique fingerprint of the data (like an ID card for data) • If even ONE character in M changes → the output H(M) changes completely • This lets you detect if data was tampered with
MethodHow it ensures Integrity
Hashing (SHA-256)Creates a unique "fingerprint" of data — any change is detected
Digital SignaturesProves the sender is genuine and data wasn't modified
ChecksumsSimple value calculated from data to detect accidental changes
Version ControlTracks every change made to a file with who changed it and when

Threats to Integrity: Man-in-the-middle attacks, SQL injection, malware modifying files, accidental errors.

3. Availability — "The system must be accessible when needed"

What it is: Ensuring that systems and data are accessible to authorized users when they need it.

How it works: Redundancy, backups, load balancing, DDoS protection.

Availability (%) = (Uptime / Total Time) × 100
• Uptime = total hours the system was working correctly (e.g., 8,730 hours) • Total Time = total hours in the period (e.g., 8,760 hours in a year) • Example: (8730 / 8760) × 100 = 99.66% availability • Industry standard is "Five Nines" = 99.999% uptime (~5 minutes downtime per year)

Threats to Availability: DDoS attacks (flooding a server), ransomware, hardware failure, natural disasters.

Extended Principles (Beyond CIA)

Non-Repudiation

Ensures someone cannot deny they sent a message or performed an action. Like a digital signature on a legal document — you can't say "I didn't sign that."

Authentication

Verifying that someone is who they claim to be. Username + Password, fingerprint scan, OTP.

Authorization

After authentication, determining what that person is allowed to do. An employee can read files but not delete them.

Accountability

Every action is logged and traced to a specific user. "Who did what and when?" — answered by audit logs.

Remember CIA = Confidentiality (who can see), Integrity (is it accurate), Availability (can you access it). These are the 3 pillars tested most often.

Cyber Threats and Attack Vectors
Simple Analogy: A threat is a burglar who might rob your house. An attack vector is how they get in — through the front door, window, chimney. In cyber security, attackers use different "ways in" (vectors) to cause harm.

What is a Cyber Threat?

A cyber threat is any potential malicious act that seeks to damage data, steal data, or disrupt digital systems.

Types of Cyber Threats

1. Malware (Malicious Software)

Software designed to harm your computer or steal data. Malware is an umbrella term covering many sub-types:

TypeWhat It DoesExample
VirusAttaches to files and spreads when the file is runILOVEYOU virus (2000)
WormSelf-replicates and spreads across networks on its ownWannaCry (2017)
TrojanDisguised as legitimate software but does harmful thingsFake antivirus apps
RansomwareEncrypts your files and demands payment (ransom) to unlockCryptoLocker
SpywareSecretly monitors and records your activitiesKeyloggers tracking passwords
AdwareForces unwanted advertisements on your screenPop-up ad injectors
RootkitHides deep in the OS, giving attacker persistent controlSony BMG rootkit (2005)

2. Phishing Attacks

Tricking users into revealing sensitive information (passwords, credit card numbers) by pretending to be a trusted source.

Spear Phishing

Targeted attack on a specific individual or company. Uses personalized info to seem more believable.

Whaling

Phishing aimed at top executives (CEO, CFO). Also called "CEO Fraud."

Vishing

Voice phishing — done over the phone. "This is your bank, please confirm your card number."

Smishing

SMS phishing — malicious links sent via text message.

3. Social Engineering

Manipulating humans (not machines) into giving up confidential information. Exploits human psychology — trust, fear, urgency.

  • Pretexting: Creating a fake scenario to extract info ("I'm from IT, I need your password to fix your account")
  • Baiting: Leaving an infected USB drive for someone to find and plug in
  • Tailgating: Physically following an authorized person into a restricted area

4. Man-in-the-Middle (MitM) Attack

Attacker secretly intercepts communication between two parties. Like someone reading your letters before delivering them.

5. Denial of Service (DoS) / DDoS

Flooding a server with so many requests it crashes and becomes unavailable to real users.

  • DoS: One computer floods a server
  • DDoS (Distributed): Thousands of compromised computers (botnet) flood a server simultaneously

6. SQL Injection

Inserting malicious SQL code into a web form to manipulate the database. Like typing ' OR '1'='1 in a login box to bypass authentication.

7. Zero-Day Attack

An attack that exploits a vulnerability that the software maker doesn't know about yet. "Zero days" because the maker has had zero days to fix it.

What is an Attack Vector?

An attack vector is the path or method an attacker uses to gain unauthorized access to a system.

Think of it as "the way in" — different entry points that attackers can exploit.

APT — Advanced Persistent Threat

A sophisticated, long-term attack where hackers quietly infiltrate a network and stay hidden for months or years to steal data continuously. Usually state-sponsored (government-backed hackers).

Risk Management Concepts
Simple Analogy: Risk management is like buying car insurance. You don't know if you'll have an accident (threat), but you know your car could be damaged (vulnerability), so you calculate the chance and cost, then take precautions (controls) to minimize loss.

What is Risk?

In cyber security, risk is the likelihood that a threat will exploit a vulnerability and cause harm to an asset.

Risk = Threat × Vulnerability × Impact
• Threat = Who or what might harm you (hacker, malware, natural disaster) • Vulnerability = A weakness that can be exploited (unpatched software, weak password) • Impact = The damage caused if the risk occurs (financial loss, data breach, reputation damage) • A high threat + high vulnerability + high impact = VERY HIGH RISK
Risk = Probability of Occurrence × Magnitude of Impact
• Probability of Occurrence = How likely is the threat to happen? (Low / Medium / High) • Magnitude of Impact = How bad would the damage be? (Minor / Major / Catastrophic)

Quantitative Risk Formulas

SLE = AV × EF
• SLE (Single Loss Expectancy) = Money lost if a specific threat occurs ONCE • AV (Asset Value) = How much the asset is worth (e.g., ₹50,00,000 server) • EF (Exposure Factor) = % of asset value lost in one incident (e.g., 0.3 = 30% damaged) • Example: SLE = 50,00,000 × 0.30 = ₹15,00,000 loss per incident
ALE = SLE × ARO
• ALE (Annual Loss Expectancy) = Expected total loss per year from a specific risk • SLE (Single Loss Expectancy) = Loss per single incident (calculated above) • ARO (Annualized Rate of Occurrence) = How many times the incident happens per year • Example: ALE = ₹15,00,000 × 2 (times/year) = ₹30,00,000 per year

Risk Treatment Options (The 4 T's)

1. Terminate (Avoid)

Stop doing the risky activity altogether. If online banking is too risky, don't offer it. (Eliminates risk completely)

2. Transfer (Share)

Move the risk to someone else. Buy cyber insurance. If breached, the insurance company pays. (Risk shifted, not eliminated)

3. Tolerate (Accept)

Accept the risk if it's too small to worry about or too expensive to fix. Acknowledge it exists and move on.

4. Treat (Mitigate)

Reduce the risk by implementing controls — install antivirus, use encryption, train employees. (Most common approach)

Governance, Risk and Compliance (GRC) Framework
Simple Analogy: Think of a school.
— Governance = The principal sets rules and direction.
— Risk = Teachers identify which students might fail or cause trouble.
— Compliance = Students follow the rules; inspectors check they do.
GRC makes these three work together as one system.

What is GRC?

GRC stands for Governance, Risk Management, and Compliance — a strategic framework that helps organizations align IT goals with business goals while managing risks and meeting legal requirements.

G — Governance

What it is: Governance defines who makes decisions about security, what the organization's goals are, and how policies are created and enforced.

How it works: Top management (Board, CISO, CEO) sets the security direction. They create policies, assign roles, and allocate budgets for security.

  • Establishing security policies and standards
  • Defining roles and responsibilities (who is accountable)
  • Setting organizational risk appetite (how much risk is acceptable)
  • Ensuring security strategy aligns with business goals

Key roles in Governance:

RoleResponsibility
Board of DirectorsOverall accountability for security
CISO (Chief Information Security Officer)Leads security strategy
Security CommitteeReviews and approves security decisions

R — Risk Management

What it is: The process of identifying, analyzing, and responding to risks that could harm the organization.

How it works: Continuous process of finding vulnerabilities, assessing threats, and implementing controls. (Detailed in Topic 4)

C — Compliance

What it is: Ensuring the organization follows all applicable laws, regulations, standards, and policies.

How it works: Regular audits, automated monitoring, compliance checklists, and reporting to regulators.

  • Internal compliance: Following your own company policies
  • External compliance: Following government laws (GDPR, HIPAA) and industry standards (PCI-DSS, ISO 27001)

Why Integrate G, R, and C?

Without integration, organizations waste effort, miss risks, and fail audits. GRC brings them together:

Role of Compliance in Cyber Security
Simple Analogy: Compliance is like traffic rules. The rules (speed limits, seatbelts) exist to reduce accidents (security incidents). Following them doesn't guarantee you'll never have an accident, but it dramatically reduces the chance. If you break the rules, you get fined (penalty).

What is Compliance?

Compliance in cyber security means following the rules — laws, regulations, standards, and policies that tell organizations how to protect data and systems.

Why Compliance is Important

  • Legal requirement: Many regulations are mandatory — breaking them leads to heavy fines (GDPR fines can be up to €20 million)
  • Protects customers: Compliance standards force organizations to protect personal data
  • Builds trust: Certified organizations are more trusted by customers and partners
  • Reduces breaches: Compliance requires implementing security controls that reduce attack surfaces
  • Avoids reputational damage: Non-compliance and resulting breaches destroy brand reputation

Key Insight: Compliance is the floor, not the ceiling. Being compliant = minimum level of security. True security goes far beyond compliance.

Security Policies and Standards

Guidelines = Suggestions (not mandatory). Like a school: Policy = "Students must be safe," Standard = "Wear ID cards," Procedure = "How to get an ID card made."

The Policy Hierarchy

A security policy is a high-level document that states management's intent on protecting information and systems. It defines:

  • What the organization wants to protect and why
  • Who is responsible for security
  • Consequences of violating the policy

Example: "All employees must protect company data and follow security guidelines."

Standards are specific, measurable requirements that implement policies. More detailed than policies.

Security Controls (Administrative, Technical, Physical)
Simple Analogy: Security controls are like the multiple security measures at an airport.
— Administrative: Rules — "No liquids over 100ml allowed" (policies, training)
— Technical: X-ray machines and metal detectors (automated systems)
— Physical: Locked doors, guards, barriers (physical barriers)

What are Security Controls?

Security controls are safeguards or countermeasures implemented to protect assets, detect threats, and respond to incidents. They come in three main categories.

1. Administrative Controls (Management Controls)

What they are: Policies, procedures, guidelines, and training that govern how people behave with respect to security.

How they work: Create rules and ensure people follow them through training and audits.

Risk AssessmentsRegular analysis of current threats and vulnerabilities

2. Technical Controls (Logical Controls)

What they are: Hardware and software mechanisms that automatically enforce security policies.

How they work: Technology does the work — monitoring, blocking, detecting threats without human intervention.

ControlDescription
FirewallFilters network traffic — blocks unauthorized connections
EncryptionScrambles data so only authorized users can read it
Antivirus / Anti-malwareDetects and removes malicious software

3. Physical Controls

What they are: Physical measures that prevent unauthorized physical access to systems and facilities.

How they work: Tangible barriers and monitoring that stop people from physically accessing hardware or restricted areas.

ControlDescription

CCTV / SurveillanceCameras recording who enters/exits facilities
Biometric AccessFingerprint, retina scan to enter restricted areas

Controls can also be classified by what they do:

Remember: Administrative = People/Rules, Technical = Technology, Physical = Physical barriers. And the 5 functions: Preventive, Detective, Corrective, Deterrent, Compensating.

Compliance Lifecycle
Simple Analogy: Compliance lifecycle is like maintaining a car. You don't just buy a car and forget it. You regularly check it (audit), fix problems (remediation), get it inspected (assessment), and repeat every year. Compliance is the same continuous cycle.

What is the Compliance Lifecycle?

The compliance lifecycle is a continuous, repeating process that organizations follow to establish, maintain, and improve compliance with laws, regulations, and standards.

It's not a one-time task — threats change, laws update, and organizations evolve. Compliance must be ongoing.

Phase-by-Phase Explanation

Phase 1: Planning

What happens: Identify which regulations and standards apply to your organization. Define the scope (which systems, data, and processes are included).

  • Determine applicable laws (GDPR, HIPAA, etc.)
  • Form a compliance team and assign roles
  • Set timeline and budget for compliance activities

Phase 2: Assessment (Gap Analysis)

What happens: Compare your current state against the required state. Find all the "gaps" — areas where you're not yet compliant.

Gap = Required Controls − Currently Implemented Controls
• Required Controls = What the regulation/standard demands (e.g., ISO 27001 requires encryption of sensitive data) • Currently Implemented Controls = What you already have in place • Gap = The difference — what you need to add or fix

Phase 3: Implementation

What happens: Close the gaps found in Phase 2. Implement missing controls, update policies, train staff.

  • Install required technical controls (encryption, MFA)
  • Write or update policies and procedures
  • Train employees on new requirements

Phase 4: Monitoring

What happens: Continuously watch systems to ensure compliance is maintained. Use automated tools to alert when something drifts out of compliance.

  • Automated log collection and analysis
  • Real-time alerts for policy violations
  • Regular vulnerability scans

Phase 5: Audit

What happens: A formal review — either internal (by your own team) or external (by a third-party auditor) — to verify compliance.

TypeWho conducts itPurpose
Internal AuditYour own compliance/audit teamSelf-check before external audit
External AuditIndependent third-party firmOfficial certification or regulatory requirement
Regulatory InspectionGovernment regulator (e.g., ICO for GDPR)Mandatory check by law

Phase 6: Remediation

What happens: After the audit finds issues (non-compliance), you fix them within a defined timeframe.

  • Prioritize fixes based on risk level
  • Document what was fixed and how
  • Re-test to confirm issues are resolved

Phase 7: Reporting

What happens: Communicate compliance status to stakeholders — management, board, regulators, customers.

  • Compliance dashboard showing status
  • Audit reports submitted to regulators
  • Exception reports for ongoing issues

Overview of Global Cyber Security Regulations
Simple Analogy: Just like countries have traffic laws (drive on the left/right, speed limits), they also have cyber laws. Different countries have different rules, but the goal is the same — keep people safe in the digital world.

Why Do Cyber Security Regulations Exist?

  • To protect citizens' personal data from misuse
  • To force organizations to implement minimum security standards
  • To create accountability — organizations must report breaches
  • To enable cross-border data flow with trust
  • To punish organizations that are negligent with security

Major Global Cyber Security Regulations

1. GDPR — General Data Protection Regulation (Europe)

Who it applies to: ANY organization in the world that collects or processes data of EU citizens

Key requirements:

  • Lawful basis for processing personal data
  • Right to be forgotten — users can request their data be deleted
  • Data breach notification within 72 hours
  • Privacy by design — security built into systems from the start

Cyber Laws and Legal Frameworks
Simple Analogy: Just as a country has traffic laws to keep roads safe, cyber laws are rules governments create to keep the internet safe. They tell you what is legal/illegal online, who is responsible when something goes wrong, and what punishment follows.

What is a Cyber Law?

Cyber law (also called IT law or Internet law) is the area of law that deals with legal issues related to the use of computers, the internet, and digital technology.

It covers crimes committed online, protection of digital data, electronic contracts, digital signatures, and intellectual property in the digital world.

Why Do We Need Cyber Laws?

  • Traditional laws (written before computers) cannot handle digital crimes effectively
  • Cybercrime crosses borders — someone in one country can attack another country
  • Digital evidence needs special legal treatment (it can be copied or deleted easily)
  • Personal data shared online needs legal protection
  • E-commerce, digital contracts, and online banking need legal validity

What is a Legal Framework?

A legal framework is the complete set of laws, regulations, and rules that govern a particular area. In cyber security, a legal framework includes:

Laws

Passed by parliament/congress. Binding on everyone. E.g., IT Act 2000, GDPR.

Regulations

Detailed rules made by government agencies under a law. E.g., RBI guidelines under IT Act.

Standards

Technical best practices. Not always legally required but widely followed. E.g., ISO 27001.

Policies

Organization-level rules that implement laws internally. E.g., company data policy.

Guidelines

Advisory documents — suggestions rather than mandatory rules.

Case Law

Judgments from court cases that set precedents for future cyber crime cases.

Information Technology Act and Amendments
Simple Analogy: The IT Act 2000 is India's "rulebook for the internet." Before this law, there were no rules for online crimes in India. The IT Act made hacking, online fraud, and spreading malware illegal, and also gave legal validity to digital signatures and e-commerce.

What is the IT Act 2000?

The Information Technology Act, 2000 is India's primary law governing cybercrime and electronic commerce. It was passed on 17 October 2000 and was based on the UN Model Law on E-Commerce (1996).

Two main goals:

  • Give legal recognition to electronic transactions, digital signatures, and e-documents
  • Make cyber crimes illegal and define punishments

IT Amendment Act 2008 — Key Additions

The original 2000 Act was updated in 2008 to address new threats. Major changes:

New Crimes Added

  • Identity theft (Sec 66C)
  • Cyber terrorism (Sec 66F)
  • Privacy violation (Sec 66E)
  • Phishing / cheating (Sec 66D)

CERT-In Created

Computer Emergency Response Team – India (CERT-In) given legal authority to respond to cyber incidents, issue directives, and collect incident reports.

Body Corporates Made Liable

Companies that negligently handle sensitive personal data can be fined (Section 43A). This forced companies to take data protection seriously.

Intermediary Liability Clarified

In 2021, new rules under the IT Act were introduced targeting social media platforms:

  • Significant Social Media Intermediaries (50 lakh+ users) must appoint a Grievance Officer, Chief Compliance Officer, and Nodal Contact Person in India
  • Must remove illegal content within 36 hours of a court/government order
  • Must trace the "first originator" of a message when required by law
  • Monthly compliance reports must be published
Key Punishment Formula
• Section 43 (Civil): Compensation ≤ ₹1 crore (paid to victim, not jail) • Section 66 (Criminal): Up to 3 years jail + ₹5 lakh fine OR both • Section 66F (Cyber Terrorism): Life imprisonment (most severe) • Section 43A (Corporate negligence): Compensation based on actual damage to affected persons
Most tested: Sec 43 (civil, no jail), Sec 66 (criminal, 3yr jail), Sec 66F (cyber terrorism = life), Sec 79 (safe harbor for intermediaries), Sec 72 (confidentiality breach). The 2008 Amendment added identity theft and cyber terrorism sections.

Data Protection and Privacy Regulations
Simple Analogy: When you go to a hospital, you share your medical history — that's your personal data. Data protection laws are rules that say: the hospital can only use that data to treat you, must keep it safe, can't sell it to advertisers, and must tell you if it gets stolen.

What is Personal Data?

Personal data is any information that can identify a specific individual — directly or indirectly.

TypeExamples
Basic IdentifiersName, address, phone number, email, date of birth
Online IdentifiersIP address, cookies, device IDs, location data
Sensitive Personal DataMedical records, biometrics, financial data, religion, sexual orientation, political views
Pseudonymous DataData where direct identifiers are replaced with codes — still personal data but lower risk
Anonymous DataData that cannot be traced back to an individual — NOT personal data, fewer restrictions

What is Data Protection?

Data protection means safeguarding personal data from unauthorized access, misuse, loss, or damage. It has two aspects:

Data Privacy (Legal)

The right of individuals to control their own data — who can collect it, use it, and share it. Governed by privacy laws (GDPR, DPDP Act).

Data Security (Technical)

The technical measures to protect data from breaches, theft, and unauthorized access. Covered by ISO 27001, NIST, etc.

Core Principles of Data Protection

Most data protection laws worldwide share these 8 fundamental principles:

#PrincipleWhat it means (simple)
1Lawfulness & ConsentYou must have a valid legal reason to collect data. Usually you need the person's consent.
2Purpose LimitationCollect data for a specific, stated purpose ONLY. Don't use it for something else later.
3Data MinimizationCollect only what you actually need. Don't hoard extra data "just in case."
4AccuracyKeep data accurate and up to date. Give people the right to correct wrong data.
5Storage LimitationDon't keep data longer than needed. Delete it when the purpose is complete.
6Integrity & ConfidentialityProtect data from unauthorized access, loss, and damage (the CIA triad again!).
7AccountabilityThe organization collecting data is responsible and must be able to prove compliance.
8Individual RightsPeople have rights: access, correct, delete, and port their data.

Data Subject Rights

Right to Access

You can ask any organization: "What data do you have about me?" They must show you.

Right to Rectification

If data is wrong, you can ask for it to be corrected.

Right to Erasure ("Right to be Forgotten")

You can ask organizations to delete your data in certain situations.

Right to Data Portability

You can ask for your data in a machine-readable format to take it to a different service.

Right to Object

You can object to your data being used for marketing or profiling.

Right to Restrict Processing

You can ask the organization to pause using your data while a dispute is resolved.

India's DPDP Act 2023 (Digital Personal Data Protection)

India's own data protection law, modelled on GDPR principles. Key points:

  • Data Fiduciary: Entity that determines why and how data is processed (like a data controller in GDPR)
  • Data Principal: The individual whose data is being processed
  • Consent required before processing personal data
  • Data Localisation: Certain sensitive data must be stored in India
  • Breach notification to Data Protection Board of India
  • Penalty: Up to ₹250 crore for serious violations
Data Protection Formula (Conceptual)
Privacy = Consent + Purpose Limitation + Data Minimization + Security + Rights • Consent = permission obtained from the individual • Purpose Limitation = use only for the stated purpose • Data Minimization = collect only what is necessary • Security = technical and organizational measures to protect data • Rights = ability of individuals to access, correct, delete their data
8 principles: Lawfulness, Purpose Limitation, Data Minimization, Accuracy, Storage Limitation, Integrity & Confidentiality, Accountability, Individual Rights. Also remember Data Subject Rights (Access, Correct, Erase, Port, Object).

General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR)
Simple Analogy: GDPR is like the world's strictest privacy bouncer. It was created by the EU and says: "If you collect ANY data about EU citizens — no matter where in the world you are — you MUST follow our rules. Break them and we'll fine you so heavily your company might not survive."

What is GDPR?

The General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) is a comprehensive data privacy law enacted by the European Union. It came into effect on 25 May 2018.

Scope: Applies to ANY organization worldwide that processes personal data of EU/EEA residents — even if the organization is located in the USA, India, or anywhere else.

Who Must Comply with GDPR?

Data Controller

What it is: The organization that decides WHY and HOW personal data is collected and processed.

Example: A hospital that collects patient records — they decide why (treatment) and how (their systems).

Responsibility: Directly accountable to GDPR. Must ensure processors comply.

Data Processor

What it is: A third party that processes data ON BEHALF of the controller.

Example: A cloud company storing the hospital's patient records.

Responsibility: Must follow controller's instructions. Also has direct GDPR obligations.

The 7 Principles of GDPR (Article 5)

Lawful Bases for Processing (Article 6)

An organization MUST have at least ONE of these 6 legal bases to process personal data:

BasisWhen to UseExample
1. ConsentIndividual has freely given, specific, informed, and unambiguous consentNewsletter subscription checkbox
2. ContractProcessing is needed to fulfill a contract with the personProcessing address to deliver a purchase
3. Legal ObligationProcessing is required by lawTax authority requiring employee salary data
4. Vital InterestsProcessing is needed to protect someone's lifeSharing medical data in emergency
5. Public TaskProcessing is for official/governmental functionsElection commission processing voter data
6. Legitimate InterestsOrganization has a genuine business need that doesn't override individual rightsFraud prevention checks

GDPR Obligations for Organizations

  • Privacy by Design: Build privacy protection into systems from the start, not as an afterthought
  • Privacy by Default: Default settings must be the most privacy-protective option
  • Data Protection Impact Assessment (DPIA): Required for high-risk processing before it starts
  • Data Protection Officer (DPO): Must appoint a DPO if processing large amounts of sensitive data or are a public authority
  • Records of Processing Activities: Must document what data you process and why
  • Data Breach Notification: Must notify the supervisory authority within 72 hours of becoming aware of a breach. If high risk to individuals, must notify them "without undue delay"

Sector-Specific Regulations
Simple Analogy: General laws (like GDPR) apply to everyone. But some industries — hospitals, banks, schools — handle very sensitive data. So they have EXTRA rules on top of general laws. Just like all drivers follow road rules, but truck drivers also need a special license and follow extra safety rules.

Why Sector-Specific Regulations?

Different industries have different types of sensitive data, different risks, and different stakes. A hospital data breach is more dangerous than a retail store breach because lives could be at risk. Sector-specific rules ensure each industry takes appropriate precautions.

International Cyber Laws and Treaties
Simple Analogy: If a criminal robs a bank in India and flees to Russia, India and Russia need an extradition treaty to get the criminal back. Similarly, international cyber laws are agreements between countries to cooperate on cybercrime — sharing evidence, extraditing hackers, and creating common standards.

Why International Cyber Laws Are Needed

  • Cybercrime is borderless — an attack originates in one country, passes through servers in 5 others, and hits a victim in yet another country
  • Without treaties, hackers can hide in countries with no cyber laws
  • Digital evidence must be legally transferred between countries
  • States need agreed-upon rules for cyber warfare and nation-state attacks

The Budapest Convention (Most Important)

Convention on Cybercrime (Budapest Convention) — 2001

What it is: The FIRST and MOST important international treaty on cybercrime. Created by the Council of Europe.

Who has signed it: 68 countries including USA, UK, Japan, Australia. India has NOT ratified it yet (2024).

Four main areas it covers:

AreaWhat it requires
Substantive Criminal LawCountries must make hacking, malware, online fraud, and CSAM criminal offenses
Procedural LawCountries must have laws to quickly preserve and share digital evidence across borders
Mutual Legal AssistanceCountries must help each other investigate and prosecute cybercrime (24/7 network)
ExtraditionCountries must extradite cyber criminals to the requesting country

Additional Protocol (2022): Allows cross-border access to electronic evidence with improved safeguards for human rights.

United Nations and Cyber Security

UN GGE (Group of Governmental Experts)

Established 2004. Group of experts from major nations that negotiates norms of responsible state behavior in cyberspace.

Key outcome (2015): 11 voluntary norms including: nations should not attack critical infrastructure during peacetime; nations should not support cybercrime from their territory.

UN OEWG (Open-Ended Working Group)

Open to ALL UN member states (not just major powers). Works on broader international cyber security norms, confidence-building measures, and capacity building for developing nations.

ITU (International Telecommunication Union)

UN agency that coordinates global telecom standards. Produces the Global Cybersecurity Index (GCI) ranking countries by their cyber security capabilities.

Tallinn Manual

Not a treaty, but an academic study on how existing international law (laws of war) applies to cyber operations. Two versions: 1.0 (2013) and 2.0 (2017). Used by NATO and governments as guidance.

ISO/IEC 27001 and 27002 Standards
Simple Analogy: ISO 27001 is like a quality certification (like ISO 9001 for products) but for information security. Getting ISO 27001 certified means your organization has built a proper security management system and an independent auditor verified it. It tells customers: "We take security seriously."

What is ISO/IEC 27001?

ISO/IEC 27001 is an international standard for establishing, implementing, maintaining, and continuously improving an Information Security Management System (ISMS).

Published jointly by the International Organization for Standardization (ISO) and the International Electrotechnical Commission (IEC).

Latest version: ISO/IEC 27001:2022

What is an ISMS?

An Information Security Management System (ISMS) is a systematic approach to managing sensitive company information so it remains secure. It includes:

  • People — roles, responsibilities, training
  • Processes — policies, procedures, risk management
  • Technology — technical controls, tools
  • Documentation — records of everything

Simple Analogy: NIST CSF is like a GPS for cyber security. What is NIST CSF?

The NIST Cybersecurity Framework is a voluntary set of guidelines, best practices, and standards published by the US National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) to help organizations manage and reduce cyber security risk.

First published: 2014. Latest version: CSF 2.0 (February 2024)

Originally for US critical infrastructure, now used globally by organizations of all sizes and sectors.

Three Components of NIST CSF

1. Core

The 6 functions + their categories and sub-categories. A set of desired cybersecurity outcomes. This is the "what to do."

2. Tiers (1–4)

  • Tier 2: Risk Informed
  • Tier 3: Repeatable
  • Tier 4: Adaptive (best)

3. Profiles

Current Profile: Your organization's cybersecurity posture today.

Target Profile: Where you want to be.

The gap between them = your improvement roadmap.

COBIT Framework
Simple Analogy: NIST and ISO 27001 focus on technical security. What is COBIT?

COBIT stands for Control Objectives for Information and Related Technologies. It is a framework for IT governance and management created by ISACA (Information Systems Audit and Control Association).

Latest version: COBIT 2019

Main purpose: Bridge the gap between IT teams and business leadership. Ensure IT creates value, manages risk, and complies with regulations — from a management and governance perspective.

COBIT 2019 — Core Concepts

6 Principles of COBIT 2019

1. Provide Stakeholder Value

IT governance exists to create value for stakeholders — owners, customers, employees, regulators. Every IT decision should serve a stakeholder need.

2. Holistic Approach

Security and governance cannot be done in silos. COBIT uses 8 "enablers" (people, process, culture, information, services, infrastructure, applications, principles) that must all work together.

3. Dynamic Governance System

Governance needs to adapt as the organization, technology, and regulations change. Not a static checklist.

4. Governance Distinct from Management

Governance (Board/executives) = EVALUATE, DIRECT, MONITOR.
Management (CIO/CISO/teams) = PLAN, BUILD, RUN, MONITOR.

5. Tailored to Enterprise Needs

COBIT is flexible — organizations pick the objectives that apply to them. A small startup uses fewer COBIT objectives than a global bank.

6. End-to-End Governance

Covers the entire enterprise IT environment — on-premises, cloud, outsourced, third-party — not just internal IT.

Compliance Requirements Mapping
Simple Analogy: Imagine you need to follow 3 different sets of rules — school rules, sports club rules, and local government rules. Many rules overlap: "Be punctual" appears in all three. Compliance mapping is the process of comparing all these rule-sets, finding what overlaps (so you don't do it three times), and identifying what's unique to each set.

What is Compliance Requirements Mapping?

Compliance requirements mapping is the process of identifying, comparing, and aligning the security controls and requirements from multiple regulations/standards that apply to your organization.

Organizations rarely follow just one standard. A hospital might need to follow HIPAA + ISO 27001 + GDPR (if treating EU patients) + RBI guidelines (if handling payments). Mapping shows what overlaps and what's unique.

Why Do Compliance Mapping?

  • Avoid duplication: Don't implement the same control 3 times for 3 different standards
  • Find gaps: Identify controls required by one standard but not yet implemented
  • Reduce cost: One control satisfying multiple requirements saves money
  • Efficient audits: Show auditors evidence that addresses multiple requirements at once
  • Unified view: Management gets one picture of compliance status across all frameworks

Regulatory Audits and Assessments
Simple Analogy: A regulatory audit is like a surprise inspection at a restaurant. The health inspector (auditor) comes in, checks the kitchen (your systems), tests the food (your controls), reviews records (your logs), and either gives you a pass certificate or orders you to fix problems. If you fail, you can be fined or shut down.

What is a Regulatory Audit?

A regulatory audit is a formal, systematic examination by an authorized auditor to verify that an organization is complying with applicable laws, regulations, and standards.

Who conducts it: Either internal auditors (your own team) or external auditors (independent firms or government regulators).

Types of Audits

Internal Audit

Conducted by the organization's own audit team. Used for self-assessment, preparation for external audits, and continuous monitoring.

Advantage: Detailed, can be done frequently, lower cost.

Limitation: May have bias (auditing your own work).

External / Third-Party Audit

Conducted by an independent certified firm. Required for ISO 27001 certification, SOC 2, PCI-DSS Level 1, etc.

Advantage: Objective, credible to regulators and customers.

Limitation: Expensive, takes time to schedule.

Regulatory Inspection

Conducted by a government regulator (e.g., ICO for GDPR, CERT-In in India, RBI for banks). Can be triggered by a breach or done routinely.

Consequence of failure: Fines, license suspension, criminal charges.

Penetration Testing (Technical Audit)

Ethical hackers attempt to break into your systems to find real vulnerabilities. Required by PCI-DSS, NIST, and many security standards.

Audit Risk Formula

Audit Risk (AR) = Inherent Risk (IR) × Control Risk (CR) × Detection Risk (DR)
$$AR = IR \times CR \times DR$$ • AR (Audit Risk) = probability that the audit fails to detect a material misstatement or non-compliance • IR (Inherent Risk) = risk that exists before any controls — how risky is the area naturally? (e.g., financial systems have high inherent risk) • CR (Control Risk) = risk that existing controls fail to prevent or detect the issue (e.g., weak password policy = high control risk) • DR (Detection Risk) = risk that the auditor's procedures fail to detect a problem (e.g., limited sampling = higher detection risk) • Goal: Keep overall Audit Risk low by testing more where IR and CR are high • Example: IR=0.8 (high), CR=0.5 (medium), DR=0.1 (low) → AR = 0.8×0.5×0.1 = 0.04 (4% chance audit misses an issue)

Cyber Risk Assessment Methodologies
Simple Analogy: Before building a house, a builder checks: what are the dangers in this area? (floods, earthquakes?) How likely are they? How bad could the damage be? And how strong should I build the walls? Cyber risk assessment is the same process — but for your digital systems.

What is a Cyber Risk Assessment?

A cyber risk assessment is a structured process of identifying what could go wrong, how likely it is, and how much damage it could cause — so you can prioritize which risks to fix first.

It answers three questions:

  • What are your assets? (What needs protection)
  • What could threaten them? (Threats and vulnerabilities)
  • What would happen if a threat succeeded? (Impact)

Core Risk Formula (Foundation)

Risk = Likelihood × Impact
$$\text{Risk} = \text{Likelihood} \times \text{Impact}$$ • Likelihood = How probable is it that the threat will occur? (Rated 1–5 or Low/Med/High) • Impact = How bad is the damage if it happens? (Financial loss, reputational damage, legal fines) • Example: Phishing attack: Likelihood = 4 (High), Impact = 4 (High) → Risk Score = 16 (Critical)

Threat Modeling
Simple Analogy: Before a bank opens, the architects ask: "Where could a thief break in? Through the front door? The vault? An insider employee?" They then design walls, cameras, and procedures to stop each entry point. Threat modeling is the same — you think like an attacker BEFORE building a system, so you can block attacks before they happen.

What is Threat Modeling?

Threat modeling is a proactive, structured approach to identifying potential threats to a system, understanding how attackers might exploit weaknesses, and designing defenses — before the system is deployed.

Key principle: "Think like an attacker to defend like a defender."

When to do it: During system design phase, before coding begins, and when major changes are made.

The 4 Key Questions of Threat Modeling

1. What are we building?

Create a diagram of the system — all components, data flows, trust boundaries, users, and external services.

2. What can go wrong?

Identify all possible threats to each component using frameworks like STRIDE.

3. What are we going to do about it?

For each threat, decide: mitigate, accept, transfer, or eliminate.

4. Did we do a good job?

Review the work — are all threats addressed? Is the model complete?

STRIDE is a mnemonic for 6 categories of threats. Each letter = one threat type:

DREAD Model — Risk Scoring for Threats

After identifying threats with STRIDE, use DREAD to score how severe each threat is:

DDamage PotentialHow bad is the worst-case damage if exploited?1 (minor) to 10 (catastrophic)

Other Threat Modeling Approaches

PASTA (Process for Attack Simulation and Threat Analysis)

7-stage, attacker-centric methodology. Aligns threats to business objectives and simulates attacks to see what a real attacker would do. Used by enterprises.

Stages: Define objectives → Define scope → App decomposition → Threat analysis → Vulnerability detection → Attack enumeration → Risk impact

Attack Trees

Diagram showing all the ways an attacker could reach a goal (root node). Branches show sub-steps needed. Like a family tree but for attack paths.

Example root: "Gain unauthorized database access" → branches: SQL injection, stolen credentials, insider threat, etc.

Vulnerability Assessment and Penetration Testing Overview
Simple Analogy:
Vulnerability Assessment = A locksmith inspects your house and makes a list of every weak lock and open window. (Finding weaknesses — no actual break-in)
Penetration Testing = You hire a thief to actually try to break in, to see which weaknesses a real criminal would use. (Actually exploiting weaknesses — controlled simulation)

What is a Vulnerability Assessment (VA)?

A Vulnerability Assessment is a systematic process of finding and classifying security weaknesses in a system — using automated tools and manual checks — without actually exploiting them.

Goal: Get a comprehensive list of known vulnerabilities so they can be patched.

What is Penetration Testing (PT)?

A Penetration Test (pen test) is an authorized, simulated cyberattack on a system where ethical hackers (penetration testers / "ethical hackers") actually try to exploit vulnerabilities to see what a real attacker could achieve.

Goal: Verify which vulnerabilities are actually exploitable and what damage they can cause.

CVSS is the industry-standard method for scoring the severity of a vulnerability from 0 to 10.

(None=worst)    — UI = Does a victim need to click something? Compliance Risk Analysis
Simple Analogy: A restaurant must follow food safety regulations. Compliance risk analysis is: "What are the chances we break a food safety rule? Which rules are we most likely to violate? What happens if we do?" Then you fix the highest-risk gaps first before the health inspector arrives.

What is Compliance Risk Analysis?

Compliance risk analysis is the process of identifying, assessing, and prioritizing risks that arise from not following applicable laws, regulations, standards, and internal policies.

It asks: "Where are we most likely to fail a compliance requirement, and what is the consequence?"

Types of Compliance Risks

Legal/Regulatory Risk

Risk of violating a law or regulation.
Example: Not notifying customers within GDPR's 72-hour window after a breach.

Consequence: Fines, license suspension, criminal charges

Reputational Risk

Non-compliance becomes public, damaging the organization's brand and customer trust.

Example: News headline: "Company X violated HIPAA — exposed 1 million patient records."

Operational Risk

Compliance gaps cause internal operational failures or inefficiencies.

Example: No access review process → former employees still have system access → data theft.

Financial Risk

Direct costs of non-compliance — fines, legal fees, remediation costs, breach settlements.

Example: GDPR fine €20M + legal fees + 6 months of remediation work.

Compliance Risk Scoring Formula

Compliance Risk Score = Likelihood of Non-Compliance × Impact of Non-Compliance
$$CRS = L_{nc} \times I_{nc}$$ • CRS (Compliance Risk Score) = overall severity of the compliance risk • L_nc (Likelihood of Non-Compliance) = probability of failing the requirement (1–5 scale)    Factors: complexity of requirement, staff awareness, existing controls, historical violations • I_nc (Impact of Non-Compliance) = severity of consequence if the requirement is violated (1–5 scale)    Factors: fine amount, criminal liability, reputational damage, operational disruption • Example: GDPR breach notification (complex, tight 72hr window) → L=4, I=5 → CRS = 20 (Critical)

Inherent Risk vs Residual Risk in Compliance

Residual Risk = Inherent Risk − Risk Reduction from Controls
$$R_{residual} = R_{inherent} - \Delta R_{controls}$$ • Inherent Risk (R_inherent) = the compliance risk that exists BEFORE any controls are in place • ΔR_controls = risk reduction achieved by implementing controls (training, monitoring, automation) • Residual Risk (R_residual) = the risk that REMAINS after controls are applied • Goal: Reduce residual risk to an acceptable level (below the organization's risk tolerance/appetite) • Example: Inherent Risk = 20; Training + automation reduces it by 14 → Residual = 6 (acceptable)

Audit Planning — Step by Step

Phase 1: Audit Universe and Risk-Based Planning

The audit universe is the complete list of all auditable areas (every system, process, department that could be audited). From this, a risk-based audit plan selects which areas to audit this year, based on:

  • Highest risk areas get audited first/most often
  • Previous audit findings (unresolved issues get re-audited)
  • Regulatory requirements (some areas must be audited annually)
  • Changes in the environment (new systems, new regulations)

Phase 2: Audit Charter and Terms of Reference

Before starting, document:

  • Audit scope: Exactly what systems, processes, locations are included (and excluded)
  • Audit objectives: What questions the audit will answer
  • Audit criteria: Which standards/regulations will be checked against (ISO 27001, PCI-DSS, etc.)
  • Resources: Auditors assigned, timeline, budget
  • Rules of engagement: What auditors are allowed to do (especially for pen tests)

Security Metrics and Key Performance Indicators (KPIs)
Simple Analogy: A hospital measures: average wait time, infection rate, patient satisfaction score. These numbers tell management whether the hospital is performing well and where to improve. Security metrics do the same — they give numbers that tell you how well your cyber security program is working, so you can improve it.

What are Security Metrics?

Security metrics are measurable values that quantify the effectiveness of your security controls, processes, and overall security program.

Key Principle (Peter Drucker): "What gets measured gets managed." Without metrics, you're guessing whether your security is improving.

Types of Security Metrics

Leading Indicators

Predict future security performance. They help you prevent problems before they occur.

Examples: % of systems patched this week; % of staff trained this month; number of open critical vulnerabilities

Lagging Indicators

Measure past security events. They show what already happened — useful for trend analysis.

Examples: Number of breaches this quarter; average MTTD last month; total security incidents this year

Process Metrics

Measure whether security processes are being followed correctly.

Examples: % of access reviews completed on time; % of incidents documented per procedure

Outcome Metrics

Measure actual security outcomes — are we achieving the desired security state?

Examples: Number of breaches; data loss incidents; successful phishing simulations caught

Most Important Security KPIs with Formulas

1. Mean Time to Detect (MTTD)

MTTD = Total Detection Time / Number of Incidents
$$MTTD = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n} T_{detect,i}}{n}$$ • MTTD (Mean Time to Detect) = average time from when an attack starts to when it is detected • T_detect,i = detection time for each individual incident i (in hours/days) • n = total number of incidents in the measurement period • Lower is better — faster detection = less damage • Example: 3 incidents with detection times of 2h, 4h, 6h → MTTD = (2+4+6)/3 = 4 hours • Industry average: ~197 days (IBM 2023 report) — target: less than 24 hours

2. Mean Time to Respond (MTTR)

MTTR = Total Response Time / Number of Incidents
$$MTTR = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n} T_{respond,i}}{n}$$ • MTTR (Mean Time to Respond) = average time from detection to containment/resolution of an incident • T_respond,i = time taken to respond to incident i • Lower is better — faster response = less damage spreads • Example: 3 incidents with response times of 1h, 3h, 2h → MTTR = (1+3+2)/3 = 2 hours

3. Patch Compliance Rate

Patch Rate = (Patched Systems / Total Systems) × 100%
$$\text{Patch Rate} = \frac{\text{Systems with patches applied}}{\text{Total systems in scope}} \times 100\%$$ • Patched Systems = systems that have the latest critical patches installed • Total Systems = all systems that should have patches applied • Target: 100% for critical patches within 30 days; 95% minimum acceptable • Example: 450 out of 500 servers patched = 90% patch rate (below target)

4. Vulnerability Remediation Rate

Vuln Remediation Rate = (Vulnerabilities Fixed / Total Found) × 100%
$$VRR = \frac{V_{fixed}}{V_{total}} \times 100\%$$ • V_fixed = number of vulnerabilities remediated within SLA (Service Level Agreement) • V_total = total vulnerabilities identified in the period • Tracked separately by severity: Critical SLA = 24 hours; High = 7 days; Medium = 30 days

Continuous Monitoring and Compliance Automation
Simple Analogy: Old approach: Once a year, someone manually checks if all the smoke detectors in a building work. New approach: All smoke detectors are connected to a central system that monitors them 24/7, sends an alert the moment any detector stops working, and automatically logs the issue. Continuous monitoring = always-on automatic checking instead of periodic manual inspection.

What is Continuous Monitoring?

Continuous monitoring is the ongoing, automated collection and analysis of security data from all IT systems to detect threats, vulnerabilities, and compliance violations in real time — instead of only during scheduled audits.

Why it's needed: Threats change daily. A system compliant at an audit might be non-compliant a week later (after a misconfiguration, a missed patch, or a new threat). Continuous monitoring catches this immediately.

Third-Party Risk Management (TPRM)
Simple Analogy: Your house is very secure — good locks, alarm system, CCTV. But you hired a cleaner (third party) who has a spare key. If the cleaner is careless or dishonest, your house is at risk despite all your own security. Third-Party Risk Management = checking that everyone who has access to your "house" (data, systems) is also trustworthy and secure.

What is Third-Party Risk Management (TPRM)?

TPRM is the process of identifying, assessing, monitoring, and managing risks that arise from relationships with external parties — vendors, suppliers, contractors, service providers, cloud providers, and partners — who have access to your organization's systems, data, or facilities.

TPRM Risk Scoring Formula

Vendor Risk Score = Inherent Risk Score × (1 − Mitigation Effectiveness)
$$VRS = IRS \times (1 - ME)$$ • VRS (Vendor Risk Score) = residual risk after considering mitigating controls • IRS (Inherent Risk Score) = base risk score based on access level + data sensitivity + business criticality (0–100 scale)    Example: Cloud provider storing all customer PII + critical to operations → IRS = 85/100 • ME (Mitigation Effectiveness) = how well their security controls reduce risk (0 to 1)    ME is derived from questionnaire scores, certifications, audit results: 0.7 = 70% effective mitigations • Example: IRS = 85, ME = 0.70 → VRS = 85 × (1 − 0.70) = 85 × 0.30 = 25.5 (Medium Risk) • Lower VRS = lower residual vendor risk. Target: All Tier 1 vendors below VRS threshold set by risk appetite.

4th Party Risk — The Hidden Threat

4th party risk is the risk from your vendor's vendors (sub-processors, sub-contractors).

Example: You use Vendor A (payroll company). Vendor A uses Vendor B (cloud storage) to store employee data. Vendor B gets hacked. Your employee data is compromised — but you had no direct relationship with Vendor B.

How to manage: Require sub-processor approval clauses in contracts; ask vendors for their own TPRM program details; use threat intelligence services that monitor 4th parties.

TPRM = managing risks from external vendors. Real examples: Target (HVAC vendor), SolarWinds (software supply chain). 4 tiers: Critical → High → Medium → Low. Key contract clauses: DPA, Right to Audit, Breach Notification. VRS = IRS × (1 - ME). 4th party = your vendor's vendors.

Design and Implementation of Security Controls
Simple Analogy: Building a new office? You plan security BEFORE construction: where do the locks go? Where are the cameras? Which rooms need key-card access? Security control design is the same — you plan and build security INTO a system, not bolt it on later when it's too late.

What is a Security Control?

A security control is any safeguard, mechanism, or action designed to protect the confidentiality, integrity, and availability of information assets. Controls prevent attacks, detect incidents, or recover from them.

Control Design Principles (The Golden Rules)

1. Least Privilege

What it is: Give users ONLY the minimum access they need to do their job — nothing more.

How: A clerk can read records but not delete them. An app only gets database read access, not write.

Why: If account is compromised, damage is limited to what that account can do.

2. Defence in Depth

What it is: Use multiple layers of security so if one fails, another catches the attack.

How: Firewall + IDS + Antivirus + Encryption + MFA — not just one control.

Why: No single control is 100% effective. Layers = attacker must break ALL of them.

3. Fail Secure (Fail Safe)

What it is: When a system fails, it defaults to the MOST secure state.

How: If the firewall crashes, it blocks ALL traffic (not lets all traffic through).

Why: A failure should never open a security gap.

4. Separation of Duties

What it is: No single person should have end-to-end control over a critical process.

How: Person A approves a payment; Person B executes it. Neither can do both alone.

Why: Prevents fraud and insider threats.

5. Security by Design

What it is: Build security requirements into the design phase — not added afterwards.

How: Threat model the system BEFORE coding. Review architecture for security flaws BEFORE building.

Why: Fixing security in production = 100× more expensive than fixing in design.

6. Economy of Mechanism

What it is: Keep security designs simple. Complex systems have more bugs and are harder to audit.

How: Don't over-engineer. A simple, well-understood control is better than a complex one nobody understands.

Control Selection — How to Choose the Right Control

Controls are chosen based on:

  • Risk assessment results — implement controls that address the highest risks first
  • Cost-benefit analysis — cost of control must be less than expected loss from the risk
  • Regulatory requirements — some controls are mandatory (GDPR encryption, PCI-DSS firewall)
  • Control catalogues — ISO 27001 Annex A (93 controls), NIST SP 800-53 (1,000+ controls)
Control Cost-Benefit Analysis
$$\text{Value of Control} = ALE_{before} - ALE_{after} - C_{control}$$ • ALE_before = Annual Loss Expectancy BEFORE implementing the control (SLE × ARO) • ALE_after = Annual Loss Expectancy AFTER implementing the control (risk reduced) • C_control = Annual cost of the control (purchase + maintenance + staff time) • If result is positive → the control is worth implementing (saves more than it costs) • If result is negative → the control costs more than it saves → consider alternatives • Example: ALE_before=₹50L, ALE_after=₹10L, C_control=₹5L → Value = 50-10-5 = ₹35L gain

Access Control and Identity Management Compliance
Simple Analogy: In a hospital, a nurse can see patient records (needs it for work), a janitor cannot (doesn't need it). A doctor can prescribe medicine, a nurse cannot. This is access control — who gets in and what they can do. Identity management = proving WHO you are before deciding WHAT you can do.

What is Access Control?

Access control is the process of restricting access to resources so only authorized users can access the right resources in the right way at the right time.

It is based on three fundamental questions:

  • Authentication: WHO are you? (Prove your identity)
  • Authorization: WHAT can you do? (What are you allowed to access?)
  • Accounting/Auditing: WHAT did you do? (Log everything for audit trail)

This is called the AAA Framework (Authentication, Authorization, Accounting).

Access Control Models

1. DAC — Discretionary Access Control

What it is: The owner of a resource decides who can access it. "Discretion" = owner's choice.

How: Like Google Docs sharing — you create a file and you decide who to share it with (view/edit/no access).

Weakness: If the owner is compromised or careless, security breaks down. Users can grant access to others.

Used in: Windows file sharing, most personal/office applications.

2. MAC — Mandatory Access Control

What it is: A central authority (not the user) sets rules about who can access what, based on security labels. Users cannot change access permissions themselves.

How: Military systems — data is labelled "TOP SECRET." Only users with "TOP SECRET" clearance can read it. No user can override this rule.

Strength: Very secure. No user can accidentally grant wrong access.

Used in: Government/military systems, SELinux.

3. RBAC — Role-Based Access Control (Most Common)

What it is: Access is granted based on the user's role in the organization, not the individual user.

How: "Nurse" role has access to patient records. "Finance" role has access to payroll. You assign the role to a user; the role determines the access.

Strength: Easy to manage — add/remove a role instead of updating 100 individual users.

Used in: Most enterprise systems, databases, cloud IAM.

4. ABAC — Attribute-Based Access Control

What it is: Access decisions are based on multiple attributes — user attributes + resource attributes + environment conditions.

How: Access only if: User is a Doctor AND User's department = Cardiology AND Patient is in Cardiology ward AND Time is 8am–8pm. All conditions must be true.

Strength: Most flexible and granular.

Used in: Modern cloud systems, Zero Trust environments.

Weakness: Can be forgotten, guessed, or stolen (phishing).

Zero Trust Architecture

Zero Trust is a modern security model based on the principle: "Never trust, always verify."

Traditional model assumed: users inside the network are trusted. Zero Trust assumes: NO user or device is trusted by default, even if they are already inside the network.

Zero Trust = Verify Explicitly + Least Privilege + Assume Breach
• Verify Explicitly = Always authenticate and authorize every request — check identity, device health, location, time of access • Least Privilege = Give only minimum necessary access; use Just-In-Time (JIT) access for elevated privileges • Assume Breach = Design systems as if attackers are already inside — segment the network, encrypt everything, monitor everything
AAA = Authentication (who are you?) + Authorization (what can you do?) + Accounting (what did you do?). 4 access control models: DAC (owner decides), MAC (labels/clearance), RBAC (role-based, most common), ABAC (attributes, most flexible). MFA = 2+ factors. Zero Trust = never trust, always verify.

Data Protection Methods

Once classified, data is protected using techniques proportional to its sensitivity:

1. Encryption

What it is: Converting readable data (plaintext) into unreadable data (ciphertext) using a mathematical algorithm and a key.

Encryption/Decryption
$$C = E_k(P) \quad \text{(Encryption)}$$ $$P = D_k(C) \quad \text{(Decryption)}$$ • P (Plaintext) = original readable data (e.g., "My password is 1234") • C (Ciphertext) = encrypted unreadable data (e.g., "a3f#9kL!mN2@p") • E_k = encryption function using key k • D_k = decryption function using key k • Symmetric encryption (AES-256): same key encrypts and decrypts — fast, used for data at rest • Asymmetric encryption (RSA-2048): public key encrypts, private key decrypts — used for key exchange, digital signatures

2. Data Masking

What it is: Replacing real sensitive data with realistic-looking fake data for use in testing/non-production environments.

Example: Real: 9876-5432-1098-7654 → Masked: 9876-XXXX-XXXX-7654

3. Data Loss Prevention (DLP)

What it is: Tools that monitor, detect, and prevent unauthorized transmission of sensitive data outside the organization.

Example: DLP blocks an employee from emailing a file containing 100 credit card numbers to a personal Gmail account.

3 types: Network DLP (monitors emails/web), Endpoint DLP (monitors USB, copy-paste), Storage DLP (monitors stored files).

4. Tokenization

What it is: Replacing sensitive data with a non-sensitive placeholder (token). The real data is stored securely in a separate "token vault."

Example: Credit card 4111-1111-1111-1111 → Token: TKN-839201. The token is used in transactions; the real card number never travels across systems.

5. Data Minimization

What it is: Collect and store only the data you actually need. Delete data when it's no longer needed.

GDPR Article 5(c) requires this as a fundamental principle.

Incident Response Planning
Simple Analogy: A fire drill. You don't wait for a fire to figure out how to evacuate. You plan the escape route, practice it, assign roles (floor warden, assembly point coordinator), and post the plan on walls. When a real fire occurs, everyone knows exactly what to do. Incident Response Planning is the cyber security equivalent of a fire drill plan.

What is a Security Incident?

A security incident is any event that actually or potentially violates the confidentiality, integrity, or availability of information assets.

Event vs Incident: Every incident is an event, but not every event is an incident. A failed login attempt = event. 1000 failed login attempts from the same IP = incident (likely brute force attack).

What is an Incident Response Plan (IRP)?

An Incident Response Plan is a documented, pre-approved procedure that defines what to do when a security incident occurs — step by step, with assigned roles and timeframes.

Each Phase in Depth

Phase 1: Preparation

Goal: Be ready before an incident happens.

  • Form a Computer Security Incident Response Team (CSIRT) — assign roles: Incident Manager, Technical Analyst, Communications Lead, Legal Counsel, HR
  • Create and maintain the Incident Response Plan document
  • Deploy detection tools (SIEM, IDS/IPS, EDR)
  • Establish communication channels (secure out-of-band comms in case main systems are compromised)
  • Run tabletop exercises and simulations to practice
  • Define what constitutes an incident and its severity levels

Phase 2: Identification (Detection & Analysis)

Goal: Determine that an incident has occurred and understand its scope.

  • SIEM/IDS alerts trigger initial investigation
  • Confirm: is this a true incident or a false positive?
  • Classify severity (P1 = Critical, P2 = High, P3 = Medium, P4 = Low)
  • Document the timeline — when did it start? What was the first indicator?
  • Identify affected systems, users, and data

Phase 3: Containment

Goal: Stop the incident from spreading further.

  • Short-term containment: Immediate actions — isolate the infected machine from the network, block malicious IP, disable compromised account
  • Long-term containment: Apply patches, change passwords, rebuild compromised systems — while keeping the environment operational
  • Preserve forensic evidence (take memory dump, disk image BEFORE wiping)

Phase 4: Eradication

Goal: Remove the root cause of the incident.

  • Delete malware from infected systems
  • Close the vulnerability that was exploited (patch, configuration fix)
  • Remove all backdoors and attacker persistence mechanisms
  • Scan all related systems to ensure no other machines were affected

Phase 5: Recovery

Goal: Restore systems to normal operation.

  • Restore from clean backups (verified pre-breach backups)
  • Reconnect systems to network after verifying they are clean
  • Monitor closely for signs of re-infection
  • Define Return to Operations (RTO) — target time to be fully operational

Phase 6: Lessons Learned (Post-Incident Review)

Goal: Learn from the incident to prevent recurrence.

  • Hold a post-incident review meeting within 2 weeks
  • Document: What happened? How was it detected? What worked? What didn't? What should change?
  • Update the IRP, detection rules, and training based on findings

Digital Forensics Basics
Simple Analogy: A detective at a crime scene: collects fingerprints, photographs everything, bags evidence carefully, ensures nothing is contaminated. If evidence is mishandled, it's thrown out in court. Digital forensics is exactly this — but the crime scene is a computer, and the evidence is logs, files, and memory dumps.

What is Digital Forensics?

Digital forensics is the process of identifying, preserving, analyzing, and presenting digital evidence in a way that is legally admissible in court.

It is used to:

  • Investigate security incidents — what happened, how, and who did it
  • Support legal proceedings — prove a cybercrime in court
  • Incident response — understand attacker's methods to improve defenses
  • Regulatory compliance — demonstrate due diligence in investigating breaches

Types of Digital Forensics

Computer/Disk Forensics

Analysis of hard drives, SSDs, USB drives. Finding deleted files, browsing history, documents, installed software.

Network Forensics

Capturing and analyzing network traffic (packets). Using pcap files, firewall logs, NetFlow data to trace attacker's network activity.

Memory (RAM) Forensics

Taking a snapshot of a running computer's RAM. Can reveal running processes, encryption keys, passwords, malware that exists only in memory (fileless malware).

Mobile Forensics

Extracting data from smartphones and tablets — messages, call logs, GPS location, app data.

Cloud Forensics

Collecting evidence from cloud services — logs, API calls, access records. Challenging because data is on provider's infrastructure.

Malware Forensics

Analyzing malicious software to understand how it works, what it does, how it spreads, and who created it.

Most Important Concept: Chain of Custody

The chain of custody is the documented, unbroken trail of evidence handling from collection to court presentation. It proves evidence was not tampered with.

It records: Who collected the evidence? When and where? Who had access to it? Was it transferred? How was it stored?

If the chain of custody is broken (e.g., evidence was left unattended, or the hash doesn't match), the evidence may be declared inadmissible in court — the entire case could collapse.

Write Blockers — Protecting Evidence Integrity

When creating a forensic copy of a hard drive, a write blocker is used — a hardware or software device that allows reading the drive but prevents ANY writes (modifications) to it. This preserves the original evidence.

Hash Verification — Proving Integrity

Forensic Hash Verification
• Step 1: Calculate hash of original evidence → H₁ = SHA256(Original Drive) • Step 2: Create forensic copy (bit-for-bit exact copy) • Step 3: Calculate hash of forensic copy → H₂ = SHA256(Forensic Copy) • Step 4: Verify: If H₁ = H₂ → copy is perfect, evidence is intact • If H₁ ≠ H₂ → the copy is different from the original → evidence integrity compromised • SHA-256 = Secure Hash Algorithm (256-bit output). A 1-bit difference in the drive = completely different hash. • All forensic analysis is done on the COPY, never the original — original is preserved as evidence

Business Continuity and Disaster Recovery Compliance
Simple Analogy:
Business Continuity (BC) = The restaurant that has a gas outage switches to a generator and continues serving customers — business doesn't stop.
Disaster Recovery (DR) = After a kitchen fire destroys the restaurant, they follow a plan to rebuild and reopen as fast as possible.
BC = keep running during disruption. DR = recover after a disruption.

Definitions

Business Continuity Plan (BCP): A comprehensive plan to ensure that critical business functions continue during and after a disaster or major disruption. Focuses on maintaining operations.

Disaster Recovery Plan (DRP): Specific procedures for recovering IT systems and data after a disaster. Subset of BCP focused specifically on IT/technology recovery.

Business Impact Analysis (BIA): The process that identifies critical business functions and determines the impact of disruption over time. Forms the foundation for BCP/DRP planning.

Key Recovery Metrics

RTO and RPO — The Two Most Important BC Metrics
$$\text{RTO (Recovery Time Objective)} = \text{Maximum acceptable downtime}$$ • RTO = the maximum time allowed for a system to be down before it causes unacceptable business impact • Example: Online banking: RTO = 4 hours. If down longer than 4 hours → unacceptable loss • Lower RTO = more expensive (need hot standby systems)
$$\text{RPO (Recovery Point Objective)} = \text{Maximum acceptable data loss}$$ • RPO = how much data (measured in time) you can afford to lose • Example: RPO = 1 hour means you can lose at most 1 hour of data. So backups must run every hour. • If RPO = 0 → no data loss allowed → real-time replication needed (expensive)
$$\text{MTD (Maximum Tolerable Downtime)} \geq \text{RTO}$$ • MTD = the absolute maximum time the business can survive the disruption before it fails • RTO must always be less than MTD — you must recover before the business collapses

Compliance as Code (CaC)

Compliance as Code is the practice of expressing compliance requirements and security policies as machine-executable code — so they are automatically enforced and verified in the DevSecOps pipeline.

How it works:

  • Compliance controls are written as code (scripts, configuration files, policy definitions)
  • These are version-controlled (Git) alongside application code
  • CI/CD pipeline automatically runs compliance checks on every code commit or infrastructure change
  • If a compliance check fails → the pipeline halts → developer is immediately notified to fix it
  • Compliance status is always up-to-date, not only at the next quarterly audit

Infrastructure as Code (IaC) Security

IaC = defining cloud infrastructure (servers, networks, databases) using code files (Terraform, CloudFormation) instead of manual configuration.

Security benefit: Infrastructure configuration is consistent, version-controlled, and automatically scanned for misconfigurations before deployment.

Common IaC misconfigurations that cause breaches:

  • S3 bucket configured as "public" (accidentally)
  • Security groups allowing 0.0.0.0/0 (all traffic) on port 22 (SSH)
  • Databases with no encryption at rest defined
  • Logging not enabled for cloud services

Shift-Left Security

"Shift left" = move security testing to earlier stages of development (to the LEFT of the timeline).

Key rule: The earlier a security flaw is found in development, the cheaper it is to fix. A bug found in design = trivial fix. Same bug found in production = catastrophic cost.

Privacy by Design and by Default

Simple Analogy: Building a house — Privacy by Design = architect adds locks and curtains WHILE designing. Privacy by Default = the front door is locked by default; guests must ring the bell (not the other way around).

Privacy by Design — 7 Principles (GDPR Article 25)

Coined by Dr. Ann Cavoukian. Build privacy INTO the system from Day 1, not as an afterthought.

1. Proactive, not Reactive

Prevent privacy issues BEFORE they occur — don't wait for a breach to add encryption.

2. Privacy as the Default

If a user does nothing, maximum privacy is still protected. Opt-out model, not opt-in.

3. Privacy Embedded into Design

Privacy is a core feature, not an add-on. Like seatbelts in a car — always there.

4. Full Functionality (Win-Win)

Privacy AND security AND usability can all coexist — no tradeoffs needed.

5. End-to-End Security

Protect data from creation to deletion. Encrypt at rest, in transit, and in use.

6. Visibility & Transparency

Users can see what data is collected and how it is used. No hidden tracking.

7. Respect for User Privacy

User-centric defaults: easy opt-out, data deletion on request (GDPR Right to Erasure).

Compliance Case Studies

Simple Analogy: Case studies are real-world examples — like court cases in law school — that teach you what happens when companies follow or break the rules.

Case Study 1 — Meta GDPR Fine (2023) — 1.2 Billion Euro

What Happened: Meta transferred EU user data to US servers without adequate protection after EU–US Privacy Shield was invalidated.
Violation: GDPR Chapter V — illegal cross-border data transfer without valid SCCs.
Fine: 1.2 billion Euro (largest GDPR fine ever). Ordered to stop EU to US transfers.
Lesson: Always have SCCs as backup. Adequacy decisions can be struck down.

Case Study 2 — Marriott Hotel Breach (2018) — 500M Records

What Happened: Hackers inside Starwood's systems since 2014. Marriott acquired Starwood in 2016 but never audited its security. Breach discovered 2018.
Violation: GDPR — failure to assess third-party risk during acquisition, inadequate security.
Fine: UK ICO fined 18.4M pounds. Reduced from 99M due to COVID.
Lesson: Mergers and acquisitions must include cybersecurity audits. You inherit the target's vulnerabilities.

Case Study 3 — Uber Data Breach Cover-Up (2016)

What Happened: Hackers stole data of 57M users and 600K drivers. Uber paid the hackers $100,000 to stay quiet and hid this from regulators for a year.
Violation: Breach notification laws — failure to notify within required deadlines.
Fine: $148M settlement. CSO Joe Sullivan criminally convicted — first US criminal conviction of a CISO.
Lesson: NEVER hide a breach. The cover-up is always worse than the incident itself.

Case Study 4 — SolarWinds Supply Chain Attack (2020)

What Happened: Russian hackers planted malware in SolarWinds Orion software update. 18,000+ organizations including US government downloaded the infected update.
Violation: Supply chain integrity failure; FISMA compliance failures; inadequate DevSecOps.
Fine: SolarWinds CISO charged with fraud by SEC. Company paid $26M settlement.
Lesson: Software supply chain security is mandatory. SCA (Software Composition Analysis) and signed releases are essential.

Cyber Insurance and Liability Management

Simple Analogy: Car insurance — you hope you never need it, but if an accident happens, you're not paying out of pocket. Cyber insurance is the same for data breaches and ransomware attacks.

First-Party vs Third-Party Coverage

First-Party (Your own losses)
  • Business interruption loss
  • Ransomware payment costs
  • Data recovery costs
  • Breach notification costs (letters, call centre)
  • PR and reputation management
Third-Party (Liability to others)
  • Legal costs if customers sue you
  • Regulatory fines (some policies)
  • PCI-DSS penalties from card networks
  • Settlement costs
  • Media liability (defamation from data leak)

Cyber Insurance Premium Formula

$$\text{Premium} = \frac{ALE \times \text{Coverage\%}}{(1 - \text{Loss Ratio})}$$

  • ALE = Annual Loss Expectancy — expected losses per year from breaches
  • Coverage % = Fraction of losses the policy covers (e.g., 80% means insurer pays 80 cents of every dollar lost)
  • Loss Ratio = Insurer's expected claims divided by premiums collected (industry average is about 65% = 0.65)
  • Example: ALE = 500,000 dollars, coverage 80%, loss ratio 0.65 — Premium = (500,000 x 0.80) / (1-0.65) = 400,000 / 0.35 = about 1.14M per year

What Insurers Look For (Underwriting Criteria)

ControlWhy Insurer CaresImpact on Premium
MFA on all accountsStops 99% of password attacksLarge discount
Regular offline backupsReduces ransomware payout riskLarge discount
Incident Response planLimits damage durationModerate discount
Security awareness trainingReduces phishing success rateModerate discount
No prior claimsLower risk profileBest rates available

Common Exclusions — What Insurance Will NOT Cover

  • Nation-state attacks (war exclusion — Lloyd's updated this in 2023)
  • Insider threats caused intentionally by executives
  • Pre-existing vulnerabilities known before policy started
  • Regulatory fines in many jurisdictions (GDPR fines often excluded)
Exam Tip: Cyber insurance does NOT replace compliance — you still get fined by regulators. Insurance covers breach costs; fines for violations are usually excluded. Insurance is risk transfer, not a compliance strategy!

Future Trends in Cyber Security Compliance

Simple Analogy: Today's compliance is like today's traffic laws. In 5 years, autonomous cars will need entirely new rules. Cyber compliance evolves as fast as the technology it governs.

Top 8 Future Trends

1. Post-Quantum Cryptography (PQC)

NIST finalized PQC standards in 2024 — CRYSTALS-Kyber (encryption) and CRYSTALS-Dilithium (signatures). Organizations must migrate from RSA and ECC before quantum computers arrive around 2030.

2. AI-Driven Regulation

EU AI Act is just the start. Every major sector — healthcare, finance, law — will need dedicated AI compliance frameworks. High-risk AI must be audited before deployment.

3. Continuous Compliance Monitoring

Annual audits are being replaced by real-time automated compliance dashboards. Tools auto-scan and report violations continuously — called CCM (Continuous Controls Monitoring).

4. Supply Chain Security Laws

After SolarWinds, EU NIS2 Directive (2024) and US Cybersecurity Executive Order require organizations to audit ALL third-party vendors and every software component they use.

5. Zero Trust as Legal Requirement

US government mandated Zero Trust architecture by 2024 via Executive Order 14028. The private sector is following. "Perimeter security is sufficient" is no longer an accepted defence in a compliance audit.

6. Privacy-Enhancing Technologies (PETs)

Differential privacy, homomorphic encryption, and federated learning allow data analysis without exposing raw personal data. These will become compliance requirements in health and finance AI.

7. CISO Personal Legal Liability

Uber CSO convicted in 2023. SolarWinds CISO charged in 2023. CISOs are now personally liable for negligence and misrepresentation. Board-level cybersecurity oversight is becoming mandatory.

8. Global Privacy Law Convergence

130+ countries now have privacy laws. The trend is toward interoperability frameworks like APEC CBPR and global adequacy decisions to reduce compliance complexity for multinational companies.

Organizational Compliance Culture and Awareness Programs

Simple Analogy: A fire extinguisher is useless if nobody knows how to use it. The best security policies fail if employees don't follow them. Culture = making security a daily habit, not just a checkbox.

Why Compliance Culture Fails

Security vs Productivity

Employees bypass controls (share passwords) because security feels like friction that slows them down.

Training Only Once a Year

Annual checkbox training — forgotten within weeks. It changes what employees know, not how they behave.

No Top-Down Support

If the CEO skips MFA or shares passwords, why would junior employees care about the policy?

Measuring Culture — Security Culture Score Formula

$$\text{Security Culture Score} = \frac{(1-\text{Click Rate}) + \text{Training\%} + \text{Reporting Rate}}{3} \times 100$$

  • Click Rate = Fraction of employees who click fake phishing links. Lower is better. (1 - Click Rate) rewards low click rates.
  • Training % = Fraction of employees who completed security training on time (e.g., 0.90 = 90%)
  • Reporting Rate = Fraction of suspicious emails that employees actually reported to security team (e.g., 0.70 = 70%)
  • Example: Click Rate 5% (so 1 - 0.05 = 0.95), Training 90% (0.90), Reporting 70% (0.70) — Score = (0.95 + 0.90 + 0.70) / 3 x 100 = 85 out of 100 — Good culture!
